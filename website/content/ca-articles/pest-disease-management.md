## 1. Core Philosophy: Build Life, Don't Kill Pests

John Valentino's approach to pest and disease management is fundamentally different from conventional practice. Rather than attacking pests and diseases with chemicals after they appear, the philosophy centers on building soil biology so robust that pest problems rarely arise in the first place.

> "The best way to eradicate pests and disease and not waste thousands of dollars is to focus on beneficial complex soil life and to build life."
> -- John Valentino

> "What I'm constantly trying to do is use things that improve the soil because it's the soil that's the most powerful way to prevent and defeat pests and diseases."
> -- John Valentino

Valentino frequently uses the analogy of a native plant community to illustrate the principle:

> "I don't know if you've been to a native plant community, let's say like a redwood forest. Why is it in a redwood forest everything looks healthy? There's no aphid. Nobody's putting on any types of pesticides or fungicides. It's a self-sustaining plant community because the soil is being fed by the native plant community and that soil is rich and that soil is full of life -- beneficial bacteria, fungi, good protozoa, good nematodes."
> -- John Valentino

### The Human Health Analogy

> "In my life here in recent years, I've started eating healthier, and by eating healthier, I'm not having to take the medications that I used to take in abundance."
> -- John Valentino

The same principle applies to plants: nourish the foundation (soil), and the need for interventions (pesticides) drops dramatically.

### Key Principles

- **Feed the soil, not just the plant.** Chemical fertilizers feed plants directly but neglect -- and often damage -- soil life.
- **Treat pests organically so you build soil while you fight pests.** Every intervention should ideally improve the soil.
- **Start with the least toxic approach.** Water blast first, then organic products, then targeted organic pesticides. Chemical treatments are a last resort.
- **A pest-free garden is not necessarily the goal.** A small number of pests actually benefits the ecosystem by attracting beneficial predators like ladybugs and parasitic wasps.

> "It's not absolutely necessary that your garden be entirely free of aphid and whitefly. A little bit here and there isn't going to hurt anything. In fact, many excellent gardeners consider it a plus because it can attract beneficials like ladybugs and parasitic wasps."
> -- John Valentino

## 2. The 70% Principle: Most Pests Start in the Soil

One of Valentino's most frequently repeated statistics is the origin of pests:

> "Up to 70% of pests start in the soil. One of the real good strategies is if you build life in the soil, those pests will be eradicated before they become pests while they're still in the soil. Beneficial microbes eat the beginning forms of pests."
> -- John Valentino

This principle is central to the entire pest management strategy:

- Most pest organisms have a soil-dwelling larval or early life stage.
- In healthy, biologically active soil, beneficial microorganisms consume these early-stage pests before they ever reach the plant.
- If you build complex soil life (bacteria, fungi, protozoa, beneficial nematodes), you create an underground defense force that works continuously without any intervention.

> "70% of pests including tomato worms start in the soil. And if you have complex beneficial soil life, it will be eating those potential pests before they become pests."
> -- John Valentino

### How It Works

1. Pest eggs or larvae enter the soil.
2. Beneficial nematodes, protozoa, and bacteria in healthy soil detect and consume them.
3. The pest never completes its life cycle and never reaches the plant.
4. This cycle is self-sustaining as long as soil life remains healthy and diverse.

## 3. Why Chemical Treatments Are Counterproductive

Valentino is emphatic that chemical treatments for pests and diseases create a destructive cycle:

> "If you don't have healthy soil and every time you get some pest problem or some disease problem and you go to the nursery and buy a toxic chemical to get rid of it, you put yourself on this treadmill of having to address disease and then damaging the soil more every time you do that, which pretty much guarantees that you're going to keep getting those pests and keep getting those diseases."
> -- John Valentino

### The Chemical Treadmill

1. A pest or disease appears.
2. The gardener applies a chemical pesticide or fungicide.
3. The chemical kills the target pest **but also kills beneficial soil organisms**.
4. With beneficial soil life damaged, the soil has **less** natural defense.
5. The pest or disease returns, often worse than before.
6. More chemical is applied, further damaging the soil.
7. The cycle escalates indefinitely.

### Case Study: Bio Advanced Fungus Control

Valentino devoted an entire video to why products like Bio Advanced Fungus Control are harmful:

> "Bio Advanced makes it sound like maybe this is some new progressive material, but when I look into it, I see it's been around since about 1981. That is about the time that synthetic fungicides started being used dramatically in home soils. So, this is just another chemical fungicide."
> -- John Valentino

The active ingredient, propiconazole:
- Inhibits soil enzyme activity
- Decreases soil dehydrogenase (DHA), a key indicator of soil microbial activity
- Is bad for pollinators, pets, and waterways
- Makes the problem more likely to reoccur

> "When you spray chemicals like Bio Advanced fungus control, you are negatively impacting that ecosystem of tremendous life."
> -- John Valentino

### Case Study: Merit and Subdue

One client garden had chronic fungal disease on fescue lawn and heavy pest problems:

> "They responded by using Merit and every year they were ordering more and more Merit and lots of synthetic fungicide -- they used Subdue -- and each year the garden would look a little bit worse in summer. So about 7 years ago we got involved and changed the protocol. Actually stopped using the Merit and the Subdue, started infusing the soil with beneficial life by using our Blend and our Penetrate Liquid Biotiller and we used our fungicide Citrol Blast. That combination... really changed the garden dramatically. Everything started looking healthier, blooming more."
> -- John Valentino

### The Weed-and-Feed Problem

> "Most weed and feeds have 2,4-D and some other chemicals for the weed killer part. 2,4-D is associated with cancer, kidney disease, reproductive problems. Studies have indicated that dog owners who use 2,4-D products, their dogs are twice as likely to develop canine malignant lymphoma."
> -- John Valentino

## 4. The Soil Food Web as Defense System

Healthy soil contains a hierarchy of life that forms a natural defense:

| Level | Organisms | Role in Pest Defense |
|-------|-----------|---------------------|
| **Level 1** | Bacteria | Lowest life form; foundation of the food web |
| **Level 2** | Fungi (including mycorrhizae) | Compete with and overpower disease-causing fungi |
| **Level 3** | Protozoa | Consume bacteria and fungi; release nutrients in plant-available form |
| **Level 4** | Beneficial Nematodes | Highest-level microorganisms; consume grubs, larvae, and early-stage pests |
| **Macro** | Earthworms | Produce castings that repel pests; aerate soil |

> "When the kind of high-level organisms eat the bacteria and fungi, that's when the nutrients are released in a plant-available form."
> -- Shannon (soil scientist, guest on the channel)

> "If you have a soil food web that's very active, you need a lot of different organisms to kind of complete that web. And if you don't have a soil food web and you're just attacking one negative organism with a bunch of pesticides, it's not going to work out in the long term."
> -- Shannon

### What Healthy Soil Looks Like Under the Microscope

Under 400x magnification, Valentino and soil scientist Shannon have observed:
- Active bacteria (green-colored, visible inside nematodes)
- Fungal hyphae growing thicker and more complex in healthy soil
- Nematodes "squirming around like crazy" -- a sign of healthy soil
- The mouth parts of nematodes reveal what they eat: beneficial nematodes eat bacteria, not roots

> "I think I found a nematode... they actually look so much larger than bacteria -- huge. The mouth parts will tell you what it eats. And if it eats roots, that's not beneficial. But this particular one eats bacteria."
> -- Shannon

## 5. Root Knot Nematodes: Identification and Treatment

### The Problem

Root knot nematodes are destructive nematodes that eat plant roots, causing galls and eventually killing plants. They are among the most frustrating pest problems.

> "Nothing's more frustrating than having bad nematodes. Nematodes that eat the roots of your plants."
> -- John Valentino

### Why Chemical Treatment Fails

> "The traditional ways of treating bad nematodes is with more and more toxic chemicals. And that usually renders the soil pretty much useless. Even if you get rid of the bad nematodes, it damages the soil so severely."
> -- John Valentino

> "When you fight root-eating nematodes with chemicals, it pretty much -- the result is dead soil."
> -- Shannon

### The Biological Solution

> "Bad nematodes are best controlled by good nematodes. And that's why building your soil with complex soil life is the way to avoid bad nematodes."
> -- John Valentino

> "If you have bad nematodes in your soil, you should build the soil with this complex life leading to good nematodes because good nematodes are stronger... and dominant over the bad nematodes that eat roots."
> -- John Valentino

Shannon confirms: "Competition is key for sure."

### Treatment Protocol for Root Knot Nematodes

1. **Stop all chemical treatments immediately.** They are making the problem worse.
2. **Apply beneficial nematodes** (purchase from Arbico Organics or similar supplier).
   - Apply in water, early morning or late evening (avoid direct sunlight).
   - Best time: early spring.
   - Frequency: once a year in mild climates (Zone 9); may need annual reapplication in cold climates.
3. **Build soil life aggressively:**
   - Apply Blend (or compost/earthworm castings) at 20-30 lbs per thousand square feet.
   - Spray Penetrate Liquid Biotiller over the top.
   - Repeat twice per year (spring and fall).
4. **Be patient.** The goal is to build a population of beneficial nematodes that permanently outcompetes the destructive ones.

> "Good nematodes are the way to go. And applying them early morning, late evening in water, fairly easy process, maybe as often as once a year in the early spring."
> -- John Valentino

## 6. Fungal Diseases: Types, Prevention, and Natural Treatment

Fungal disease is the single most common lawn and garden problem Valentino encounters in Zone 9 Fresno.

### Types of Fungal Disease

- **Lawn fungal disease** -- Most common on cool-season grasses (tall fescue) in hot summer climates. Appears as brown spots with a pattern to the discoloration.
- **Rust** -- Common on lawns in fall/winter. Treat by increasing nitrogen to help the lawn outgrow it.
- **Mold on citrus** -- Common on lemon trees and other citrus.
- **Rose fungal diseases** -- Including black spot and similar issues.

### The Two Conditions That Cause Fungal Disease

> "Fungal disease is prevalent when two conditions occur: heat and moisture."
> -- John Valentino

### Three Free Steps to Prevent Fungal Disease

**1. Water at the right time**

> "We don't want water standing around. When you water at night, it stands around until the sun comes out. A better strategy, if it's possible, is to water right when the sun is coming up or right before the sun comes up."
> -- John Valentino

**2. Fix your irrigation system for even coverage**

> "Head-to-head coverage: not where one sprinkler sprays to the spray of another sprinkler, but the spray of one sprinkler actually sprays to the next sprinkler."
> -- John Valentino

**3. Water as little as possible**

> "Water standing around is what creates fungal disease in the summer."
> -- John Valentino

### Diagnosing Fungal Disease vs. Watering Problems

| Indicator | Likely Watering Problem | Likely Fungal Disease |
|-----------|------------------------|----------------------|
| Soil moisture | Dry when probed | Wet/saturated |
| Recovery time | Improves in 1-2 weeks with watering correction | Takes months to improve |
| Pattern | Random dry spots | Distinct pattern of discoloration |
| Grass type | Any | Cool-season grass in hot climate is most susceptible |
| Soil type | Light/sandy soil | Heavy/clay soil (retains moisture) |

### Why Chemical Fungicides Make It Worse

> "Don't reach for a fungicide. That'll pretty much put you on a schedule where you get fungal disease every year because you are damaging the only natural mechanism to make sure you don't get fungal disease every year. The natural mechanism to make sure you don't get fungal disease every year is life in the soil. Beneficial fungi way stronger than the bad fungus that causes fungal disease."
> -- John Valentino

> "If you spray it with a fungicide, it kills the bad fungus, also kills all the good fungi. And by killing the bad fungus and the good fungi, you pretty much guarantee that every year you're going to have the same problem because the only way in a sustainable way to not get fungus on cool-season grass in the summer is to grow good fungi that are way stronger than bad fungi."
> -- John Valentino

### The Biological Solution to Fungal Disease

> "The good fungi that we're building are usually way stronger than these problematic disease-causing fungi. So, what we want to do is build healthy life in the soil with beneficial fungi, beneficial bacteria, and those will defeat the weaker disease-causing ones."
> -- John Valentino

### Treatment Protocol for Fungal Disease (Lawn)

1. **Correct watering immediately.** Water early morning. Ensure head-to-head sprinkler coverage. Water as little as possible.
2. **Apply Blend heavily** -- 20-30 lbs per thousand square feet for known fungal problems.
3. **Spray Penetrate Liquid Biotiller** over the Blend.
4. **Apply Citrol Blast** (natural antifungal) -- 2 shots per 1,000 sq ft mixed in 4 gallons of water, sprayed with a backpack sprayer.
5. **Apply Nourish Biosol** -- which is itself antifungal.
6. **Do NOT apply synthetic fungicides** (Subdue, Bio Advanced, etc.).
7. **Monitor and repeat** -- Results come faster than with chemical fungicides, and the solution is permanent.

> "This will cure fungal disease more permanently and actually more quickly. I've gotten very quick results from immediately infusing the soil with life instead of using a fungicide."
> -- John Valentino

### Case Study: Chronic Fungal Disease Lawn

> "In this very lawn I'm standing on, year after year they had more and more serious fungal disease in their cool-season turf in hot Fresno. They were using Subdue -- more and more Subdue every year -- and every year the problem was worse because they were creating a soil environment with less and less beneficial life. Eventually cut out all the Subdue, no more Subdue. Now are able to grow along without fungal disease as long as they can keep the watering proper in the middle of the summer."
> -- John Valentino

## 7. Aphids and Whitefly: The Earthworm Castings Method

### Immediate Response

> "The most harmless way to get rid of aphid and whitefly -- the first thing to try is with a blast of water. Sometimes that'll work, especially if it's not a major infestation."
> -- John Valentino

### Valentino's Favorite Long-Term Method

> "My favorite way is not a very easy way but it's fantastic for soil, and that is to use our Blend around the root ball and then on top of the Blend use about 2 inches of earthworm castings like Worm Gold Plus. Tremendously effective for whitefly and aphid."
> -- John Valentino

**The Earthworm Castings Mulch Method:**

1. Apply Blend (or compost) around the root zone of the affected plant.
2. Layer approximately 1.5 to 2 inches of high-quality earthworm castings over the Blend as a mulch.
3. Spray Penetrate Liquid Biotiller over the top.

This method works because:
- It builds the soil while treating the pest problem.
- Earthworm castings create conditions that repel aphids and whitefly.
- The effect is long-lasting and self-sustaining.
- It does not damage beneficial insects.

### Quick Knockdown (When Needed)

> "If you're trying to just immediately kill some aphid with something you spray on, our Enviral Repel 40 works pretty well. Number of oils and botanicals in there -- one of the main active ingredients that's really effective is garlic oil. But it's not as good a long-term solution as improving the soil."
> -- John Valentino

### Companion Planting for Aphid Prevention

> "Cilantro is fantastic to keep pests away. Aphid and whitefly don't like the smell of cilantro. Other thing about it is it's great at attracting pollinators."
> -- John Valentino

### Why Organic Control Matters for Aphids

> "We not only want to not damage the soil but we also don't want to damage the ecosystem because in many cases, including this one, one of the best ways to control aphid and whitefly is with beneficials. We don't want to be damaging those, which a lot of chemical products do, and so you end up with this kind of antiseptic ecosystem."
> -- John Valentino

### The Ant Connection

> "Usually ants aren't a primary problem -- they're a secondary problem. They are a problem because they eat the waste of aphid or the waste of white flies or the waste of other insects. If you get rid of the aphid and the whitefly and other problem insects, usually the ants will go away."
> -- John Valentino

## 10. Species-Specific Pest Issues

### Roses

Roses are among the most pest-prone plants in the California garden.

**Common problems:** Rose weevil, rose slugs, sawflies, aphid, whitefly, fungal disease.

**Valentino's rose treatment protocol:**
1. Apply 3 cups of Blend around the root zone after pruning (early spring).
2. Spray Penetrate Liquid Biotiller over the Blend.
3. Layer 2 inches of earthworm castings over the Blend.
4. If pest/disease issues persist, use Rose and Soil Defender.
5. Apply beneficial nematodes in early spring for rose weevil.

> "That combination of Blend, Penetrate Liquid Biotiller, and earthworm castings is the best thing I've come across for minimizing pest and disease and maximizing vitality of rose plants."
> -- John Valentino

**Rose weevil specifically:**
- Most prevalent on white and yellow roses.
- Best treated with beneficial nematodes applied in early spring.
- Plant deeply colored roses to avoid the problem.

> "If you don't want to have rose weevil, plant deeply colored roses and you won't have rose weevil."
> -- John Valentino

### Lemon and Citrus Trees

**Common problems:** Mold, rust, aphid.

> "Lemons can be subject to pest and disease like mold, rust, aphid. All of those things can be discouraged by healthy soil."
> -- John Valentino

**Treatment:** Same soil-building protocol as other plants -- Blend over root zone, Penetrate Liquid Biotiller sprayed over, earthworm castings as mulch for aphid issues.

### Crepe Myrtles

> "The only problem sometimes is they tend to get aphid. Feed your soil. Make your soil as healthy as possible. And usually you can control that."
> -- John Valentino

### Western Redbud (Cercis occidentalis)

**Common problems:** Grasshoppers (strip foliage in spring), army worms.

> "That is a problem with this type of redbud... Grasshoppers love it. Each spring they strip it. Might be army worms -- I see that on these all the time."
> -- John Valentino

### Tomatoes

**Common problems:** Caterpillars, tomato worms, army worms, blossom end rot (calcium issue).

**Treatment:**
- Hand-pick visible caterpillars immediately.
- Spray BT (Bacillus thuringiensis) -- safe for fruits and vegetables.
- Use Enviral Repel 40 once a week until damage stops.
- Spray Penetrate Liquid Biotiller weekly on the foliage and soil.

> "70% of pests including tomato worms start in the soil. And if you have complex beneficial soil life, it will be eating those potential pests before they become pests."
> -- John Valentino

### Cool-Season Lawns (Tall Fescue)

Most susceptible to fungal disease in Zone 9 summers.

> "You combine heavy soil, lots of water, and a cool-seasoned grass like tall fescue and that's just a perfect environment for fungal disease."
> -- John Valentino

**Bermuda grass** is far less susceptible to fungal disease but will not grow in shade.

### Fruit Trees (Plum Cot, Pluot, Peach)

Susceptible to army worms. Treat with hand-picking + spinosad + BT.

## 11. Seasonal Pest Calendar for Zone 9

### Early Spring (February - March)

| Task | Details |
|------|---------|
| **Apply beneficial nematodes** | Purchase from Arbico Organics; apply in water early morning or late evening; once per year is often sufficient in Zone 9 |
| **Spring soil building** | Apply Blend (10-20 lbs/1000 sq ft) + spray Penetrate Liquid Biotiller; best time to start the annual soil-building process |
| **Rose treatment** | 3 cups Blend per rose bush right after pruning + Penetrate Liquid Biotiller + 2 inches earthworm castings |
| **Pest products** | Generally NOT needed in spring; pest/disease problems develop as weather warms |
| **Inspect for army worms** | Check redbud trees and fruit trees for early damage |

> "Spring is the best time to start this process of building the soil... most likely I wouldn't apply [pest products] in the spring unless I saw a problem. And usually in spring there's not a lot of pest and disease problems -- those develop as we get into warmer weather."
> -- John Valentino

### Late Spring (April - May)

| Task | Details |
|------|---------|
| **Monitor for aphid/whitefly** | Common on roses, crepe myrtles, citrus |
| **Plant cilantro** | Natural aphid and whitefly repellent through scent |
| **Begin watching lawn** | As temperatures rise, watch for early fungal symptoms |
| **Caterpillar watch** | Inspect tomatoes and fruit trees for caterpillar damage |

### Summer (June - August)

| Task | Details |
|------|---------|
| **Fungal disease peak** | Most critical period for cool-season lawns; water early morning only |
| **Apply Citrol Blast** | If fungal disease is present; 2 shots/1000 sq ft in backpack sprayer |
| **Nourish Biosol** | Apply 12 lbs/1000 sq ft; it is antifungal and feeds the lawn |
| **Do NOT overfertilize** | Synthetic fertilizer in summer increases pest/disease susceptibility |
| **Second soil application** | Mid-summer Blend + Penetrate for gardens with ongoing problems |
| **Aphid/whitefly treatment** | Apply Blend + earthworm castings mulch if problems appear |
| **Grasshopper control** | Hand-pick; try Penetrate Liquid Biotiller spray |

> "More chemical fertilizer can make your lawn more susceptible to fungal disease."
> -- John Valentino

### Fall (September - November)

| Task | Details |
|------|---------|
| **Fall soil application** | Blend + Penetrate Liquid Biotiller; second annual treatment |
| **Clean garden debris** | Remove old seed pods, old fruit -- these are sources of pest and disease |
| **Rust on lawns** | Common in fall; treat by increasing nitrogen (grow it out) |
| **Mulch evaluation** | Maintain 2-inch layer of mulch; protects soil through winter |
| **Pre-emergent timing** | If needed, apply Dimension around January 15 - February 1 (Zone 9) |

### The Protocol in Practice

**For a garden with NO current problems:**
- Apply Blend once per year (spring) + Penetrate Liquid Biotiller once per year.
- Maintain 2-inch mulch layer year-round.
- Water properly (early morning, as little as possible, even coverage).

**For a garden WITH pest/disease problems:**
- Apply Blend twice per year (spring + fall), heavy rate (20-30 lbs/1000 sq ft).
- Spray Penetrate Liquid Biotiller over the Blend.
- Add earthworm castings (2-inch layer) for aphid/whitefly issues.
- Apply beneficial nematodes once per year in early spring.
- Use Citrol Blast for fungal problems.
- Use Enviral Repel 40 or BT/Spinosad for insect problems.
- Stop all chemical pesticides, fungicides, and synthetic fertilizers.

**For a garden in CRISIS (severe fungal disease, heavy pest infestation):**
- Apply Blend at 25-30 lbs per 1,000 sq ft.
- Spray Penetrate Liquid Biotiller heavily.
- Apply Citrol Blast for fungal issues.
- Apply beneficial nematodes.
- Consider adding a third application mid-summer.
- Fix irrigation system for even coverage and early-morning watering.
- Be patient -- expect 3-6 months for significant improvement.

## Summary of Core Principles

1. **Healthy soil is the best pesticide.** Build complex microbial life in your soil and most pest problems will never develop.
2. **70% of pests start in the soil.** Beneficial microbes consume early-stage pests before they ever reach your plants.
3. **Good fungi are way stronger than bad fungi.** Build beneficial fungal populations and they will out-compete disease-causing fungi.
4. **Good nematodes are stronger than bad nematodes.** Build soil life to support beneficial nematode populations that outcompete root-eating nematodes.
5. **Chemical treatments damage the very thing that protects you.** Every chemical fungicide or pesticide applied damages beneficial soil life, making future problems more likely.
6. **Every treatment should build soil.** Choose organic products that benefit the soil while addressing the pest problem.
7. **Prevention through soil health is cheaper than treatment.** Twice-yearly applications of soil-building products cost less than constant chemical interventions.
8. **Be patient and consistent.** Results come in 3-6 months, but the solution is permanent rather than requiring annual escalation.
